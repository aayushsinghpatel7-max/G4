The Rise of Digital Distribution

Before the mid-2000s, launching a video game was a monumental task. Developers had to secure a publisher, who would then handle the expensive and complex logistics of manufacturing physical discs or cartridges, printing boxes, and shipping them to retail stores around the world. These "gatekeepers" held all the power, and shelf space at stores like GameStop or Walmart was limited and expensive.

This system made it nearly impossible for small, independent developers with creative but niche ideas to ever get their games in front of players.

A New Gateway: Steam

Launched by Valve Corporation in 2003, Steam began as a simple tool to update their own games like *Counter-Strike*. However, it quickly evolved into a full-fledged digital storefront for PC gaming. This was a revolution for several reasons:

  • No Manufacturing Costs: Games were just data. Developers didn't need to print a single disc.
  • Infinite Shelf Space: A digital store can host thousands of games, from AAA blockbusters to tiny one-person projects.
  • Direct Access: Developers could publish updates and patches instantly without a publisher's approval.
  • Global Reach: A developer in Sweden could sell their game to someone in Japan with the click of a button.

Platforms like Steam, and later GOG.com, dismantled the old gatekeeper system on PC. For the first time, small teams could build a game, publish it themselves, and find a global audience, paving the way for the "indie boom."

While Steam opened the doors, it soon created a new problem: discoverability. With thousands of new games releasing every year, just getting noticed in the digital crowd became (and remains) a major challenge for developers.
