The Xbox One Launch

The launch of the Xbox One in 2013 marked a difficult start to the eighth generation for Microsoft. The company's vision was not for a simple game console, but an "all-in-one" entertainment hub for the living room, with heavy TV integration and a mandatory Kinect 2.0 sensor.

Initial Policy Controversies

"Always-On" Connectivity

Microsoft initially announced that the console would require an internet connection to function. It needed to perform a mandatory online "check-in" at least once every 24 hours, even to play single-player, disc-based games.

Digital Rights Management (DRM)

Alongside the connectivity requirement, the console featured strict Digital Rights Management (DRM) policies. These rules heavily restricted the ability to sell, trade, or even lend used physical games, in a push towards an all-digital future.

The 180-Degree Reversal

The backlash from the gaming community was immediate and overwhelming. Players and press alike criticized the policies as anti-consumer. In response to the intense criticism and Sony's effective counter-messaging, Microsoft announced a complete reversal of these policies just months before launch, removing the 24-hour check-in and all used-game restrictions.

However, the damage was done. This, combined with a $100 higher price point than its competitor (due to the mandatory Kinect), cost Microsoft significant consumer goodwill and led to the PlayStation 4 taking a commanding early lead in the console race.
