Hardware Components

Engineering the Arcade Experience

Arcade machines were early examples of powerful, single-purpose computers. They were built to be strong, reliable, and profitable. Every part of the cabinet — from the controls to the screen — was designed to create an exciting experience for players and to survive heavy use in public spaces.

Cabinets: Durability and Visibility

The arcade cabinet protected the game’s hardware and made it stand out in a crowded arcade. Cabinets came in different shapes and styles:

  • Upright Cabinets: Tall machines designed for standing play.
  • Cocktail Cabinets: Low, table-style with a flat screen under glass; screen could flip for a second player.
  • Deluxe or Environmental Cabinets: Larger, themed designs like car cockpits or flight simulators, giving a more immersive experience.

Cabinets were made from strong wood and reinforced plastic to handle years of use and occasional rough treatment.

Controls: Early Human-Computer Interaction

The controls were the main way players interacted with arcade games. They had to be simple, responsive, and tough enough for constant play.

  • Joysticks: 4-way or 8-way, with small switches giving a satisfying “click.”
  • Buttons: Large, colorful, on/off actions for fast reactions.
  • Trackballs and Spinners: Used in games like Missile Command or Arkanoid for precise movement.

Displays: CRT Technology

The screen was one of the most important parts of the arcade machine.

  • CRT Monitors: Bright, glowing screens with sharp contrast and instant reaction times, perfect for action games.

There were two main types of graphics:

  • Raster Graphics: Grid of colored pixels, used in Pac-Man and Donkey Kong.
  • Vector Graphics: Lines drawn directly by the monitor’s electron beam; games like Asteroids had a sharp, wireframe look.

Coin Mechanisms: The Business Behind the Game

The coin mechanism controlled how players paid to start a game.

  • Used sensors to check coin size, weight, and material; if valid, added a credit to the PCB (the machine’s “brain”).
  • The 1980s JAMMA standard allowed arcade owners to connect controls, screens, and PCBs through a single connector, making it easy to swap games without buying a new machine.
