Key Titles

Pac-Man, Street Fighter II, Mortal Kombat, Tetris, Asteroids

Pac-Man (1980)

Pac-Man introduced a bright, non-violent, character-driven design to arcades and attracted a much broader audience to game centers. The simple 'eat-and-escape' loop and iconic character turned it into a pop culture symbol. Designer Toru Iwatani aimed to offer an alternative to the era's space-and-shoot trends. (funfare)

Street Fighter II (1991)

Capcom's SFII set the standard for fighting games with a six-button layout, a diverse cast, and a combo system that evolved from a discovery into a core design pillar. It placed head-to-head competition at the heart of arcade economics and helped seed a global tournament culture. (capcom)

Mortal Kombat (1992)

Using digitized actors and controversial finishing moves, Mortal Kombat became a sensation and also a lightning rod. Public debates in 1993-94 in the United States led to the establishment of the ESRB ratings system in 1994, a milestone for transparency and parental guidance. (wikipedia)

Tetris (1984/85)

Created by Alexey Pajitnov in Moscow, Tetris proved how far pure mechanics can travel. Its simple rules, platform agility, and later mobile-era resurgence made it one of the most widely ported and recognized games ever. (Tetris)

Asteroids (1979)

Atari's vector-graphics classic sold tens of thousands of cabinets and became a benchmark of the clear and survive score-chasing loop. Thrust-based movement and fragmenting asteroids delivered a crisp, skill-forward arcade experience. (Asteroids)
