User Experience & Interaction Design

The Cabinet, Controls, and the Social Dimension

Playing in an arcade wasn’t just about the game on the screen — it was about the whole experience. The cabinet design, controls, and social setting all worked together to make arcade gaming exciting, interactive, and memorable.

Ergonomics of Control: Joysticks and Buttons

Most arcade machines had a joystick and a few large, colorful buttons. These controls were strong and built to handle intense, competitive play. Players could feel every move through the joystick’s clicks and button presses, giving a sense of power and control. The simple, on/off nature of these controls made them perfect for quick, reaction-based games like Street Fighter or Galaga.

Specialized Controls

Some games needed special controllers to match their themes. Racing games like Pole Position used steering wheels and pedals, while Centipede used a trackball for fast, precise movement. Later, rhythm games like Dance Dance Revolution introduced dance pads, where players moved their whole bodies. These creative control systems made arcade games more immersive and fun — something home consoles couldn’t fully copy at the time.

Cabinet Artwork and Marquees: Artwork and Immersion

The outside of the cabinet was just as important as the game itself. Bright, colorful artwork covered the sides and front, showing off the game’s characters, theme, and energy. The marquee — the lighted sign at the top — displayed the game’s title so players could spot it from across the room. These visuals helped build excitement and made each machine feel unique and alive.

Durability and Design

Arcade cabinets were made from strong wood and plastic so they could survive years of heavy use. Their sturdy design also made them easy to move and repair, which was essential for busy arcades.

The Social Aspect: Crowds and Spectators

Arcades were social spaces. When someone was doing well in a game, a crowd would often gather to watch. People would cheer, give advice, or react loudly when something exciting happened. This made playing feel like a performance and added extra pressure and thrill for the player.

Turn-Taking and Competition

Players used a simple system to show who was next — they’d place a coin or token on the machine’s edge. This small act created a friendly, face-to-face competition. Players not only wanted to beat the game but also to beat each other’s scores right then and there. The arcade became a place to meet people, compete, and share in the fun of gaming together.
