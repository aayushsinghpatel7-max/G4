Developers, Hardware & Regional Arcade Ecosystems

Atari, Namco (later Bandai Namco), Capcom, and SEGA didn't just make games; they shaped hardware, cabinet typologies (upright, cocktail, sit-down racing), and the location-based business model. Cultural differences between Japanese game centers and American mall arcades also influenced how players gathered and played.

Atari

Founded in 1972 by Nolan Bushnell and Ted Dabney, Atari helped scale the coin-op business from Pong through Asteroids and beyond, establishing the template for the cabinet-and-coin economy. (Atari)

Namco → Bandai Namco

Starting in amusement machines in 1955 and exploding globally with Pac-Man in 1980, Namco later merged with Bandai in 2005 to form Bandai Namco. The company now manages a vast portfolio across arcades and home platforms. (Namco)

Capcom

With roots in 1979 and early arcade hardware, Capcom shifted to software in the 1980s and globalized the fighting genre through Street Fighter and the CPS hardware ecosystem. (capcom)

SEGA

Evolving through the late 1950s and adopting the Sega Enterprises name in 1960, the company innovated with coin-op attractions like Periscope and, later, AM teams that pushed 3D arcade hardware in the 1990s (e.g., Virtua Fighter, Daytona USA). (SEGA)
