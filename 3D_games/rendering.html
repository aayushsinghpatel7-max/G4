3D Game Rendering — The Transition Years

This page summarizes how 3D rendering evolved during the transitional era when games moved from 2D and sprite-based techniques to real-time 3D (late 1980s — late 1990s).

Historical context

Early arcade and home games relied on sprites and 2D tricks. The move to true 3D was driven by faster CPUs, dedicated graphics chips, and new consoles/PC cards (PlayStation, Sega Saturn, Nintendo 64, 3dfx Voodoo).

Core techniques

  • Software rasterization: CPU-driven triangle rasterizers.
  • Fixed-function pipeline: Hardware for texture mapping and basic lighting (Gouraud).
  • Texture & MIP-mapping: Reduced aliasing and improved perf on limited GPUs.
  • Z-buffering: Per-pixel depth testing for correct occlusion.
  • Geometry culling: BSP, portals and LOD to manage polygon budgets.

Hardware milestones

Early 1990s: software renderers and console custom chips. Mid-1990s: 3D accelerators (3dfx). Late-1990s: mature fixed-function GPUs and standardized APIs (Direct3D, OpenGL).

Legacy

The era fixed rasterization as real-time standard and shaped art/gameplay styles (low-poly, texture atlases).
