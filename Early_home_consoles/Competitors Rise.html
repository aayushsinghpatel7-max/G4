RISE OF COMPETITORS (1977–1983)

From 1977 to 1983, given the successes that reached consoles in the 1970s, the home console market recorded an amizing growth. In fact, after Magnavox Odyssey and Fairchild Channel F, new consoles started to enter homes around the world, therefore outside the United States. These machines added color graphics, sound, microprocessors, and interchangeable cartridges, transforming video games from a curiosity into a cultural phenomenon.


1977 — Atari VCS (Atari 2600)

The Atari 2600 helped to rech the first importants peaks of success related to video-games. Its success is in fact largely due to the use of removable cartridges and joystick controls, a huge novelty for the time, which allowed it to offer hundreds of titles including Space Invaders, Adventure, and Pac-Man.

Highlights

  • 8-bit MOS 6507 processor
  • Introduced home versions of arcade successes (Pong)
  • More than 30 million units sold worldwide

1978 — Bally Astrocade

The Bally Astrocade (originally Bally Professional Arcade) adressed at technology "fans", because it was equipped with programmable cartridges and a built-in BASIC interpreter.

Highlights

  • Advanced graphics and color for 1978
  • Additional keyboard accessory for programming
  • Influenced later game/computer systems

1979 — Mattel Intellivision

Mattel’s Intellivision directly challenged Atari with considerable improvement and a unique numeric keypad controller. Its slogan, “Intelligent Television" reflected its more strategic games and simulated sports.

Highlights

  • 16-bit graphics chip
  • First console with downloadable games
  • Sports gameplay (Baseball, NFL Football, etc.)

1982 — Vectrex (Built-In Display)

The Vectrex was the first home console who had its own monitor, displaying vector-based graphics. This gave its games, like Mine Storm and Scramble, a new and bright arcade look.

Highlights

  • Vector display (not TV-based)
  • Joystick with four buttons
  • Innovative, but expensive and short-lived

1982 — ColecoVision

The ColecoVision offered a quality close to arcade and was sold with Donkey Kong. In fact, it helped Nintendo’s characters to reach millions of households for the first time.

Highlights

  • Z80 CPU (same Central Processing Unit as many arcade machines)
  • Atari 2600 compatibility
  • 2 million units sold

Between 1977 and 1983, over twenty home consoles were released. The most successful established industry standards, like interchangeable cartridges, dedicated processors, and big game libraries. Then independent studios such as Activision and Imagic started creating games on their own, marking a revolutionary step in the video game industry.

Global Expansion

The competition was not more limited olny to the United States. In fact, in Japan, companies like Epoch launched the Cassette Vision (1981), while in Europe were released systems like the Interton VC 4000 and the Philips Videopac G7000 (also denominated the European Odyssey). Each region began to experiment with its own controllers, cartridges, and marketing, all the companies wanted to achieve the perfect combination to create the best console.


In 1983 the market was booming, but dangerously saturated. In fact, the more consoles were released, with hundreds of games and inconsistent quality, the closer an inevitable collapse was coming.
