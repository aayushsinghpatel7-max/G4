RISE OF COMPETITORS (1977–1983)

From 1977 to 1983, given the successes that reached consoles in the 1970s, the home console market recorded an amizing growth. In fact, after Magnavox Odyssey and Fairchild Channel F, new consoles started to enter homes around the world, therefore outside the United States. These machines added color graphics, sound, microprocessors, and interchangeable cartridges, transforming video games from a curiosity into a cultural phenomenon.

1977 — Atari VCS (Later: Atari 2600)

The Atari 2600 helped to rech the first importants peaks of success related to video-games. Its success is in fact largely due to the use of removable cartridges and joystick controls, a huge novelty for the time, which allowed it to offer hundreds of titles including Space Invaders, Adventure, and Pac-Man.

Highlights

  • 8-bit MOS 6507 processor
  • Introduced home versions of arcade successes (Pong)
  • More than 30 million units sold worldwide

1978 — Bally Astrocade

The Bally Astrocade (originally the Bally Professional Arcade) aimed at tech enthusiasts. It featured programmable cartridges and a built-in BASIC interpreter for hobbyists.

  • Advanced graphics and color for 1978
  • Keyboard add-on for programming
  • Influenced later hybrid game/computer systems

1979 — Mattel Intellivision

Mattel’s Intellivision directly challenged Atari with improved graphics and a unique numeric-pad controller. Its slogan, “Intelligent Television,” reflected its more strategic gameplay and sports simulations.

  • 16-bit graphics chip (superior to Atari)
  • First console with downloadable games (via PlayCable)
  • Strong sports lineup: Baseball, NFL Football, etc.

1982 — Vectrex (Built-In Display)

The Vectrex was the only home console with its own monitor, displaying vector-based graphics instead of raster pixels. This gave its games, like Mine Storm and Scramble, a sharp, glowing arcade look.

  • Vector display (not TV-based)
  • Analog joystick with four buttons
  • Innovative, but expensive and short-lived

1982 — ColecoVision

The ColecoVision offered near-arcade quality and shipped with Donkey Kong, helping Nintendo’s characters reach millions of households for the first time.

  • Z80 CPU (same as many arcade machines)
  • Expansion modules for Atari 2600 compatibility
  • Approx. 2 million units sold
Between 1977 and 1983, over twenty home consoles appeared. The most successful established industry standards: interchangeable cartridges, dedicated processors, and strong game libraries. Third-party developers like Activision and Imagic began producing games independently — a revolutionary change in the business model.

Global Expansion

The competition wasn’t limited to the United States. In Japan, companies like Epoch launched the Cassette Vision (1981), while Europe saw systems like the Interton VC 4000 and the Philips Videopac G7000 (the European Odyssey²). Each region experimented with its own controllers, cartridges, and marketing.

By 1983, the market was booming — but dangerously saturated. With multiple consoles, hundreds of games, and inconsistent quality, an inevitable collapse was approaching.
