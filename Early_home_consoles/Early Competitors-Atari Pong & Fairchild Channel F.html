BOOTING SYSTEM...

Early Competitors

In the seventies, the world of video games was still in its early days. In fact, following the first experiments with the Magnavox Odyssey, new protagonists begin to enter the market, bringing ideas and technologies destined to revolutionize electronic entertainment. Among the first and most important competitors stood out Atari, with the iconic Pong game, and Fairchild, with the revolutionary Fairchild Channel F,, the first console to use interchangeable cartridges.

Atari Pong Consoles

Release

In June 1972 Atari released Pong, the first video game to achieve large-scale success. This happens mainly thanks to simplicity and accessibility, in fact Pong is often described as a social phenomenon, because the game quickly spread among bars, arcades and public places, involving children, adults, families, in a nutshell, people of all ages.

However, in the first years it was only distributed in an arcade version, in fact only in 1975, three years after the realease, it started to enter homes for the first time, since before then televisions were not yet compatible with game technology. More precisely, it occurred on August 3, 1975, when Atari, in collaboration with the Sears chain, launched a home console, which could be connected to the television. Thanks to this new format, production costs were greatly reduced and the video game became accessible to an even larger audience. Since the arcade version already had enormous popularity, as soon as the console was released the success was immediate, Pong in fact became the most desired gift of Christmas 1975 in the United States, reaching over 150,000 units sold, something that had never happened before, at the time.

Console

The Pong's home console was a fairly simple device overall, but the turning point was the ingenuity with which it was designed. In fact, it had a solid and squared body, with the particularity of having two knobs (called paddles) that allowed players to move their racket. The game, conceived and developed by electronics engineer Allan “Al” Alcorn based on a design by Nolan Bushnell, as mentioned previously, represented a home version of the original arcade.

The machine used an Atari LSI (Large Scale Integration) chip, a compact version of the arcade version, which allowed it to contain costs and make the product suitable for the domestic market.

Gameplay

Pong, as can be deduced from the name, has often been defined as "the electronic version of table tennis". In fact, on the black and white screen, appear two vertical bars, that are the rackets, and a ball that bounces between them. Each time a player fails to respond, so it fails to hit the ball with their "racket", the other earns a point. The score, shown on the screen, makes the challenge even more competitive. The game, developed by Alcorn under the supervision of Bushnell and Ted Dabney, was inspired by Table Tennis, one of the games featured on the Magnavox Odyssey.

Impact

Pong's success led to the birth of a real family of home consoles, such as Super Pong, Ultra Pong and many others. The diffusion of equivalent chips, started a spread of clones, thousands of official and non-official versions, produced by companies around the world, including Coleco, Nintendo and even the italian Zanussi. Pong represented a turning point for the entire gaming industry. The idea of bringing a video game into homes forever transformed the way of understanding entertainment. It not only paved the way for home consoles, but also demonstrated that video games could be a social and family experience. Today, more than five decades after its release, Pong remains one of pop culture's most recognizable icons and a symbol of the technological innovation.

Fairchild Channel F

Release

The Fairchild Channel F (also called Channel Fun) was produced by Fairchild Semiconductor, and launched in August 1976 at a price of $169.95. Its most important feature is that it was the first home console to use interchangeable cartridges , thus giving rise for the second generation of consoles. Developed by engineer Jerry Lawson, the system represented a revolutionary step, in fact for the first time the game code was not fixed in the console, but contained in programmable ROM “plug-in” cartridges. This innovation made it possible to create and distribute new titles without producing a new console.

Console

The Channel F introduced many new features for the time, such as a pause button that allowed you to stop the game and change settings such as time and speed, without losing the score. They also made the controllers detachable, and with a futuristic design for the time, i.e. handy cylindrical joysticks capable of rotating like paddles and moving in eight directions. Even if the system had not a commercial success, these technical solutions influenced subsequent consoles.

Games

The console came with two titles pre-installed, but during its time on the market, 27 official cartridges, costing approximately $20 each, called Videocarts, were released. Each of which contained one or more games, including for example Tennis and Hockey, and also advanced variants of classic Pong. In addition, some experimental versions involved the use of additional accessories such as keyboards, but these designs were abandoned with the license being switched.

Impact

Despite numerous innovations, the Channel F was quickly overtaken by the Atari VCS (known as the Atari 2600), which took over the market due to a richer catalog and increased power. In 1979 ,Fairchild sold the brand to Zircon International, which retained the rights until 1983. Although not having had a great commercial impact, the console remains an unalandmark in the history of video games for having introduced the concept of a programmable cartridge console.
