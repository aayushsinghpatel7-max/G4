Early Competitors: Coleco Telstar & Sears

The Rise of Competition

After the success of the Magnavox Odyssey and Atari’s Pong, dozens of companies rushed to enter the growing home console market. Among the most notable early competitors were Coleco and Sears, both of whom aimed to bring gaming to a wider audience with affordable, accessible systems.

Coleco Telstar: The pong clone mania

Released in 1976, the Coleco Telstar was designed as an affordable alternative to Atari’s home Pong. It featured a simple design with two paddle controllers and offered multiple built-in Pong variants including tennis, hockey, and handball. Powered by the General Instrument AY-3-8500 chip, the Telstar became a commercial success, leading Coleco to produce over a dozen variants in just a few years.

Each new version introduced small improvements: color output, gun controllers, and different game combinations. However, the oversaturation of the market would later contribute to the industry’s instability at the end of the decade.

Sears Tele-Games: The Branded Approach

Sears, the American retail giant, entered the gaming scene by rebranding and selling Atari’s Pong console under its own name: Sears Tele-Games. This partnership gave Atari access to Sears’ huge distribution network, reaching households that might never have bought directly from a specialized electronics brand.

Sears also sold exclusive versions of later Atari consoles, including the Sears Video Arcade, a rebranded Atari 2600. This collaboration played a key role in spreading home gaming to mainstream consumers, transforming it from a niche hobby into a cultural trend.

Legacy of the First Competitors

  • Coleco Telstar: Demonstrated the commercial potential of simple home systems.
  • Sears Tele-Games: Proved that branding and retail partnerships could define market success.
  • Industry Impact: Set the stage for the 1977–1978 “console boom,” inspiring dozens of similar machines.

Why They Mattered

The early rivalry between Coleco and Sears illustrated how quickly the home console market evolved. These companies not only fueled public interest but also laid the groundwork for future giants like Atari, Nintendo, and Sega. Their experimentation, both in design and marketing, shaped the business strategies that would define the gaming industry for decades.
