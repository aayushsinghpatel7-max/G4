ECONOMIC & CULTURAL CONSEQUENCES

Between 1972 and 1983, home consoles achieved such popularity that they went from being a risk to an important global industry. Until overnight it all collapsed, and reshaped the way games were designed, sold and even perceived by society.


The Economic Expansion (1977–1982)

Like we saw in the "Competitors Rise" section, the end of the 70s brought an explosion of new consoles and arcades. Companies like Atari, Mattel, and Coleco made millions selling consoles and cartridges. In fact, f a few years, video games were the most popular product in technology sector.



  • In 1982, video game revenues in the United States were estimated at $3.2 billion
  • Thanks to consoles, cartridges have become a recurring source of income for companies
  • Marketing focused on family fun and arcade at home experiences
After years, for the first time, video games were finally seen as a source of entertainment, not just a technological curiosity.


The 1983 Video Game Crash

In 1983, came the collapse mentioned above, sales fell nearly 97% in North America. Companies like Atari, Mattel, and Coleco have lost millions. The warehouses were full of unsold cartridges and many were literally buried in the New Mexico desert by Atari.

The collapse of 1983 brushed away most American console manufacturers. In fact, investors called video games a passing trend and the industry risked disappearing forever.


Cultural Consequences

Despite the financial disaster, the "first era" left profound cultural marks that transformed the identity of gaming:

  • Introduction of the video game as a domestic activity, families played together
  • Creation of the first "fan culture" through magazines and TV ads
  • It began early debates about screen time and the direction youth culture took
  • Encouraged technical development


These years have transformed video games into a form of pop culture, as if a bridge had been created between technology, entertainment and art.


The Impact of the First Generation

The crash of 1983 paved the way for new ideas. In the same year, in Japan, Nintendo launched Family Computer (Famicom), which was renamed Nintendo Entertainment System (NES), as we know it today. With stricter quality control and a focus on gameplay, NES saved and relaunched the entire industry in the following years.



1972–1983 built the foundations of the modern gaming: birth oh home consoles, cartridges, game genres, and the concept of “gamers”.
