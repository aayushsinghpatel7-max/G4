MAGNAVOX ODYSSEY (1972)

.

The Magnavox Odyssey was the first home videogame console, released in August 1972 and designed by Ralph H. Baer, considered in fact the “Father of Home Video Games.” It connected directly to a TV and opened a new era of entertainment.

How It Worked

  • system of interchangeable cards (ancestors of cartridges) to select different games
  • absence of sound and color graphics
  • came with TV screen overlays for visuals, cards and dice
The Odyssey didn't have much of a commercial success because the concept of a home video game was so new that many consumers struggled to understand how it worked, and this limited the diffusion of the console. But it still opened the door for future consoles, showing that playing on a television could be interactive.

Games

The console offered several titles included, including Table Tennis, who was the most popular game of the console, and also the one that the game inspired Pong. Between 1972 and 1973, were released 24 video games on Odyssey, most of all about sports or action.

Consequences

After Pong's success, Magnavox sued Atari for patent infringement, winning the case. In the following years, the company did the same with other giant brands such as Coleco, Mattel and Activision, always obtaining positive results.

In 1985, Nintendo attempted to invalidate Baer's patents, claiming that the first video game was Tennis for Two (1958). However the court established that that game did not use video signals and therefore could not be considered a real “video game”.

Despite its technical limitations, the Magnavox Odyssey laid the foundation of the modern gaming industry. It showed that electronic gaming could be a shared, domestic experience, paving the way for all subsequent consoles, like the Atari 2600 or the Nintendo Entertainment System.
