Neural & Haptic Interfaces

As immersion grows, the interface must move closer to the body — from controllers and gloves to eye, hand, and even brain signals. Haptics brings touch into digital worlds; neural I/O seeks direct communication with the nervous system.

Haptics Today

  • Vibration & HD rumble: precise motors create textures and impacts (gamepads, handhelds).
  • Force feedback: steering wheels, exoskeletons, and resistive triggers simulate weight and tension.
  • Wearables: vests and gloves deliver spatial patterns and pressure for presence cues.

Eye, Hand & Voice Tracking

Inside‑out cameras and depth sensors map hands; eye tracking enables foveated rendering and natural UI; voice fills gaps for accessibility and speed.

Design rule: avoid “gorilla arm.” Mix brief gestures, eye selection, and voice to reduce fatigue.

Neural Interfaces (BCI)

Non‑invasive BCIs (EEG, fNIRS) read brain activity through the scalp to control cursors or trigger actions. Invasive BCIs (implants) aim for higher bandwidth and therapeutic use (restoring movement, communication).

Design Guidelines

  1. Prefer multi‑modal input (hands + gaze + voice + haptics) for robustness.
  2. Give predictable feedback (visual + haptic) for every action.
  3. Respect comfort limits (field of view, motion, weight, privacy).
  4. Protect biometric data (eye movement, EMG/EEG) with opt‑in and on‑device processing where possible.
