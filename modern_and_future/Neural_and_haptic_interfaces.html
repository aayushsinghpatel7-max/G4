Neural & Haptic Interfaces

As immersion grows, the interface must move closer to the body — from controllers and gloves to eye, hand, and even brain signals. Haptics brings touch into digital worlds; neural I/O seeks direct communication with the nervous system.

Haptic Technology Nowadays

USC Scientists Create Wearable for Natural Digital Interaction

Touch plays a vital role in human connection, fostering trust, emotional bonds, and reducing stress. Yet in today’s digital world, where screens mediate most interactions, it’s often missing. To bridge this gap, researchers at the USC Viterbi School of Engineering developed a wearable haptic system that lets users exchange gestures like pats, handshakes, and squeezes in virtual reality — and feel them in real time. The system uses gloves and sleeves with small vibration motors to simulate pressure and movement, making virtual interactions more engaging, pleasant, and realistic. “People spend much of their time socializing online, yet experience rising ‘touch starvation,’” said Heather Culbertson, USC Viterbi associate professor. “Our goal is to make virtual communication reflect the emotional benefits of real-world experiences.”

How it works

The system supports up to 16 users at once, each represented by a full-body 3D avatar mirroring real movements in a shared virtual environment. Unlike video calls, users can move freely, interact with objects, and complete tasks together. “This project was born from a simple, deeply human desire — to feel closer to the people we miss,” said Premankur Banerjee, a doctoral student at USC Viterbi. To add touch, users wear gloves and armbands with vibration motors that simulate pressure and motion, allowing them to feel gestures and object interactions in VR. Tests showed participants felt a stronger sense of presence and connection with tactile feedback. “Our team combines computer science, engineering, and psychology to create technology that supports natural, emotionally meaningful virtual interactions,” said Heather Culbertson, USC Viterbi associate professor.

Eye, Hand & Voice Tracking

Inside-out cameras and depth sensors map hands; eye tracking enables foveated rendering and natural UI; voice fills gaps for accessibility and speed.

Design rule: avoid “gorilla arm.” Mix brief gestures, eye selection, and voice to reduce fatigue.

Neural Interfaces (BCI)

Non-invasive BCIs (EEG, fNIRS) read brain activity through the scalp to control cursors or trigger actions. Invasive BCIs (implants) aim for higher bandwidth and therapeutic use (restoring movement, communication).

Design Guidelines

  1. Prefer multi-modal input (hands + gaze + voice + haptics) for robustness.
  2. Give predictable feedback (visual + haptic) for every action.
  3. Respect comfort limits (field of view, motion, weight, privacy).
  4. Protect biometric data (eye movement, EMG/EEG) with opt-in and on-device processing where possible.
